01 · Executive Summary

Two ways to fail, one way through.

Most architecture debates get framed as "simple vs. complex." That framing is wrong. There are two real failure modes, and the right plan avoids both.

Under-engineering

Ship it raw, patch it live

Financial logic sits on the client. There's no idempotency, no rate limiting, no fallback when a dependency goes down. It works perfectly — right up until 10,000 people open the app at the same time.

Over-engineering

Build for traffic you don't have

Kubernetes, Kafka, a microservice mesh — for three developers and a few thousand users. The team spends its runway operating infrastructure instead of shipping the product that needs to find its market.

◆

The new plan is neither. It's a thin backend, one region, no queues, no clusters — but with money math where money math belongs: on the server, behind an idempotency check, tested in CI. It costs a little more than doing nothing. It costs a lot less than doing too much.

02 · The Plans

Same product. Different spine.

Toggle between the two architectures below. Everything the user sees stays the same — what changes is who's allowed to touch the money.

Architecture

No layer between the phone and the database.

Strengths

  • Extremely fast MVP development
  • Very low infrastructure cost
  • Minimal operational burden
  • Small-team friendly — one or two developers can run it

Weaknesses

  • Financial calculations partly inside the client
  • No centralized business logic
  • Limited observability into failures
  • Harder to maintain as rules change
"A user with a decompiled build can read your goal formula. On this plan, they can also submit their own answer to it."
Architecture

Every write to money passes through FastAPI + an RPC function.

Strengths

  • Production-grade reliability without a platform team
  • Financial logic centralized in Postgres RPC — one source of truth
  • AI isolated so its outages never touch money math
  • Real observability: Sentry, PostHog, GitHub Actions
  • Still runs on 1–3 developers, still cheap

Trade-offs

  • More moving parts than a pure Supabase app
  • A backend now exists, so someone owns deploying it
  • Slightly slower week one, in exchange for no rewrite later
"No Kubernetes. No Kafka. No microservices. No Redis, initially. Just the money logic in the right place."

Who's responsible for what, in the new plan

▲

React Native

UI, forms, charts, user interaction. Nothing that decides an outcome.

▲

FastAPI

Validation, idempotency, external APIs, merchant mapping, AI orchestration, notifications.

▲

Postgres RPC

Financial scores, goal calculations, savings logic, reward rules, monthly summaries.

▲

Trigger.dev

Weekly reports, notifications, AI insights, cleanup tasks — off the request path.

On AI: it generates explanations only. It never performs financial calculations, goal computations, business decisions, or savings formulas. If the AI service is down, the numbers are still correct.

03 · Request Flow

One tap. Two very different journeys.

Trace what happens the moment a user logs an expense.

Existing Flow

  1. 1
    User

    Taps "Save expense"

  2. 2
    Client validation

    Runs on the phone, in JS anyone can read

  3. 3
    Client calculations

    Score, streak, and goal math computed on-device

  4. 4
    Database

    Accepts whatever the client sends, gated only by RLS

  5. 5
    AI

    Called directly — if it hangs, the screen hangs

  6. 6
    Notifications

    Fired client-side

New Flow

  1. 1
    User

    Taps "Save expense"

  2. 2
    Cloudflare

    WAF + rate limiting before the request reaches your servers

  3. 3
    FastAPI validation

    Rejects malformed or hostile requests

  4. 4
    Idempotency check

    A retried tap can't create a second row

  5. 5
    Postgres RPC

    The only place score and goal math is allowed to happen

  6. 6
    Database transaction

    Commits atomically, or not at all

  7. 7
    Trigger.dev worker

    Handles anything that doesn't need to block the tap

  8. 8
    AI explanation

    Adds color commentary — never blocks the save

  9. 9
    Notification delivery

    Sent from the backend, retried if it fails

05 · In Plain Language

Four ways to explain this to anyone.

🍳

The restaurant kitchen

In the existing plan, every customer walks into the kitchen and seasons their own plate. In the new plan, orders go through a line cook who follows the recipe every time — the customer still picks the dish, they just don't touch the stove.

🗼

The airport control tower

Existing plan: every pilot radios the runway directly and hopes nobody lands at the same time. New plan: a tower coordinates, so collisions are structurally prevented — not just less likely.

🏦

The bank teller

No bank lets a customer write their own ledger entry and hand it over for filing. Every deposit is checked, logged, and reconciled by the bank first. That check is the FastAPI + RPC layer.

🚑

The emergency room

A hospital doesn't let one overwhelmed patient intake desk decide who gets seen. Triage, monitoring, and escalation exist so one bad moment doesn't become a systemic failure — that's what Sentry and rate limiting do here.

The real cost of the existing plan at 10,000 users isn't the invoice — it's the incident. A duplicated payout or a corrupted goal balance costs far more in trust than a FastAPI instance costs in dollars.

07 · Scaling Journey

Evolve on purpose, not under pressure.

Each phase is a real checkpoint, not a hard deadline — reassess when traffic tells you to, not before.

01

Foundation

React Native, FastAPI, Supabase, Postgres RPC. Money logic server-side from day one — this is the one step that isn't optional.

02

Operate

Trigger.dev for background work, Sentry for errors, PostHog for product analytics. Now you can see what's actually happening.

03

Scale reads

Redis for hot, repeated reads. A Postgres read replica to take reporting load off the primary.

04

Harden deploys

Blue-green deployments. Instant rollback. This is where you stop being able to take an app-store-length outage.

08 · Final Recommendation

Ship the new plan.

Not because it's impressive. Because it's the smallest architecture that still tells the truth about a user's money, every time.
