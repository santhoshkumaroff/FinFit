01 — Executive Summary

Neither extreme wins. The hybrid does.

FinFit handles people's money. That single fact changes which shortcuts are safe to take — and which ones become liabilities the day usage spikes.

01

The existing plan is right for a demo, not a ledger

React Native + Supabase gets FinFit into users' hands in days. But financial math running on the client means every number on screen is a claim the server never checked. That's fine for a prototype. It's not fine for a balance.

02

The production plan is correct — and too much, too soon

A FastAPI layer, background workers, read replicas, and isolated AI services are exactly what FinFit needs at 10,000 concurrent users. Built on day one, they're also months of engineering the MVP doesn't have time for.

03

The hybrid wins because it sequences the risk correctly

Ship on Supabase for speed, but move financial calculations and business logic server-side from day one. Everything else — Cloudflare, workers, read replicas, observability — layers in as a scheduled migration, not a rewrite under pressure.

Why this matters

A UI bug shows the wrong color. A financial logic bug shows the wrong balance. FinFit only gets to be wrong about the first kind.

      05 — In Plain Terms

      Why a backend layer matters, without the jargon

      The Restaurant Kitchen

      A diner never walks into the kitchen to grab their own steak off the grill. A server takes the order, the kitchen prepares it under supervision, and quality is checked before it reaches the table. The existing plan lets the customer cook in the back. The production plan keeps a kitchen between the request and the result.

      The Airport Control Tower

      Planes don't land whenever they want, in whatever order they arrive. A control tower sequences them, holds some back, and clears others — so nothing collides on a single runway. Direct client-to-database writes are planes landing with no tower. Idempotency checks and rate limiting are the tower.

      The Banking System

      A bank never lets a teller update your balance directly from a note you handed them — a back office validates, logs, and reconciles every transaction before it's final. That's what server-side financial logic does for FinFit: the balance you see is the balance the system verified, not the balance the app calculated.

          08 — Cost Analysis

          What each plan costs, at scale

          Over-engineering on day one isn't just wasted effort — it's cash that should have gone to distribution, sitting idle in infrastructure nobody needs yet.

          Why over-engineering early is dangerous

          Building read replicas and dedicated workers for 1,000 users adds real monthly cost and operational surface area for a load your infrastructure could handle on a single Supabase instance. Sequence the spend to match the traffic, not the ambition.

          09 — Final Recommendation

          🏆 The recommended architecture

          Cost

          Infrastructure spend scales with actual usage, not projected usage.

          Reliability

          Financial logic runs server-side, verified, and idempotent from day one.

          Developer productivity

          Supabase's speed is kept for everything that isn't money-critical.

          Scalability

          Workers and read replicas absorb load spikes without touching the API contract.

          Security

          Cloudflare and server-side validation stand between the internet and the ledger.

          Production readiness

          Sentry and PostHog mean failures are visible before users report them.

          FinFit should evolve from MVP to production the way real money should be handled: deliberately, in verified steps, with every calculation happening somewhere it can be checked.
